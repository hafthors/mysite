GET INSPIRED BY EINSTEIN


Visit his summerhouse in Caputh by Potsdam. Caputh is indeed a "stone's throw" from Berlin, especially when you consider it's just south of Potsdam and easily accessible. It's a wonderful example of the beautiful and tranquil escapes you can find so close to the bustling capital. It's a very impressive and inspiring experience to. Many are touched when they visit the house. It is a special feeling to be able to be in Einstein's summer house.




The house offers a unique insight into where one of the greatest minds of the 20th century relaxed, thought, and spent time with family and friends. It was his "paradise," a place where he could escape the world and enjoy nature, hiking, and sailing. Historical Significance: Einstein lived there from 1929 to 1932, a pivotal time in his life before he was forced to flee Germany due to the rise of the Nazis. The house itself has a rich and sometimes turbulent history, including expropriation during the Nazi era. Architectural Interest: The house was custom-built for Einstein in 1929 by the renowned architect Konrad Wachsmann. It's a wooden house that was designed to align with Einstein's personal needs, taking advantage of the sloping property. Peaceful Atmosphere: Situated on the edge of a forest with views of Lake Templin, the location itself is incredibly serene and offers a sense of the tranquility Einstein cherished. Connection to Great Minds: Einstein hosted many notable figures from the worlds of science and art at his summerhouse, including Max Planck, Erwin Schrödinger, Heinrich Mann, and Käthe Kollwitz. What to expect when visiting: The summerhouse is in Caputh, a small village about 6 kilometers south of Potsdam. It's easily accessible by bus from Potsdam Hauptbahnhof. Opening Hours: The Einstein House is typically open from April to October, on Saturdays, Sundays, and public holidays, from 10:00 AM to 6:00 PM. Guided tours usually start on the hour. Tours: While the rooms are largely empty, you can often take a guided tour (some in English during high season) that provides details about Einstein's life in the house, the architecture, and its history. English documentation may also be available. Exhibitions: There's also a permanent exhibition on "Einstein's Sommer-Idyll in Caputh" at the Caputh Bürgerhaus (community center), which offers more context about Einstein's life, work, and the history of the house, with models, photos, and documents. Surroundings: Beyond the house, the area is beautiful for walks or hikes, reflecting Einstein's love for long strolls and sailing on the nearby lakes. It's a relatively simple house, but its significance and the stories it holds make it a truly inspiring place to visit for anyone interested in history, science, or simply finding a peaceful retreat.
