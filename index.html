Questioning and Curiosity: Einstein emphasized the importance of never stopping to question. His passionate curiosity was a driving force throughout his life, leading him to challenge established scientific paradigms.
Persistence: He famously said, "It's not that I'm so smart, it's just that I stay with problems longer." He was incredibly persistent in wrestling with difficult concepts until he found a solution.
Intuition and Imagination: While rigorous in his scientific methods, Einstein highly valued intuition and imagination. He felt that "Logic will get you from A to B. I magination will take you everywhere." He believed that imagination was even more important than knowledge, as it stimulates progress and gives birth to evolution.
Thought Experiments (Gedankenexperimente): This was perhaps his most iconic tool. Instead of relying solely on physical experiments, Einstein would construct elaborate scenarios in his mind to explore complex scientific ideas. Famous examples include imagining himself riding alongside a beam of light (which led to special relativity) or a person falling in an elevator (which helped him develop general relativity). These mental visualizations allowed him to grasp the implications of his theories in intuitive ways.
Combinatory Play: He believed in the power of connecting seemingly unrelated ideas. He often drew inspiration from diverse fields, including music (he was an accomplished violinist) and philosophy, to approach problems in physics from fresh perspectives. He called this "combinatory play," and it was central to his creative process.
